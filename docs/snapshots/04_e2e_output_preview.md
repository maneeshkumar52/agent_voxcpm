**Renewable Energy: Technologies, Challenges & Future Outlook (2035)**
============================================================

Introduction
------------

Renewable energy refers to the generation of power from natural resources that can be replenished over time, such as solar, wind, hydro, geothermal, and biomass. The transition to renewable energy is crucial for mitigating climate change, ensuring energy security, and promoting sustainable development.

**Renewable Energy Technologies**
-------------------------------

### Solar Energy

Solar energy is generated from the sun's rays, either through photovoltaic (PV) panels or concentrated solar power (CSP) systems. PV technology converts sunlight into electricity, while CSP systems use mirrors or lenses to focus sunlight onto a heat exchanger, generating steam that drives turbines.

### Wind Energy

Wind energy is generated by harnessing the kinetic energy of wind using turbines. Onshore and offshore wind farms can be used to generate electricity, with onshore installations being more common.

### Hydro Energy

Hydro energy is generated from the movement of water in rivers, oceans, or tidal currents. Large-scale hydroelectric power plants use dams to store water, which is then released through turbines to generate electricity. Small-scale hydro systems, such as run-of-river schemes, can also be used to generate power.

### Geothermal Energy

Geothermal energy is generated from the heat of the Earth's core. Direct use applications include heating and cooling buildings, while geothermal power plants convert steam into electricity.

### Biomass Energy

Biomass energy is generated from organic matter such as wood, crops, and waste. Biomass can be burned to produce heat or electricity, or converted into biofuels for transportation.
