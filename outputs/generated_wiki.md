# Renewable Energy: A Practical Wiki Overview

Renewable energy is energy generated from naturally replenishing sources such as sunlight, wind, moving water, and geothermal heat. It is central to reducing greenhouse gas emissions, improving energy security, and supporting long-term economic resilience.

## Core Sources

### Solar
Solar energy converts sunlight into electricity through photovoltaic systems and utility-scale solar plants.

### Wind
Wind turbines transform kinetic wind energy into electrical power, with both onshore and offshore deployments.

### Hydropower
Hydropower uses flowing water to generate electricity and remains one of the largest renewable contributors globally.

### Geothermal
Geothermal power taps heat beneath the Earth surface and offers stable baseload generation in suitable regions.

### Biomass
Biomass uses organic materials for heat, fuels, and electricity; sustainability depends on feedstock sourcing and lifecycle impact.

## Grid and System Challenges
- Intermittency and storage requirements
- Transmission buildout and modernization
- Demand response and smart grid coordination
- Permitting and land-use complexity

## Policy and Investment Trends
Governments are combining tax incentives, renewable portfolio standards, and industrial policy to accelerate deployment. Private capital continues to flow into utility-scale generation, storage, and enabling software.

## Outlook to 2035
Renewables are expected to capture a growing share of new capacity additions. The biggest differentiators will be storage economics, transmission expansion, and policy consistency.

## Conclusion
Renewable energy is moving from an alternative option to a structural foundation of modern energy systems. Successful adoption depends on technology, policy, market design, and execution quality.
